Milestone 2: Definition and Related Work

A. Task/Problem Definition

  1. Introduce your problem

    2. The problem our group came up with is the disorganized nature of having multiple Hybrid Synchronous Classes. In a Hybrid Synchronous format, this would amount to 4 separate zoom links and 4 different scheduled cohort times if a student took 4 classes. This is an issue for both instructor and students. Students often find themselves wasting time hunting for zoom links or finding their designated cohort assignments through hundreds of emails or through ELC. The confusing issue of cohorts is further exacerbated if the class rotates it's schedule of designated times for groups of students to come. This indirectly affects an Instructor's ability to teach if students are facing obstacles in joining class online and in-person.

    • Goals
      1. Goal (form the goal) - The goal is to attend class either in-person based on our scheduled cohort time or access the zoom links to attend online.
    • Execution
      1. Plan (the action) - Get the zoom links ready to attend class online or find the scheduled cohort time and attend class in-person.
      2. Specify (the action sequence) - We must login to eLC or check our mail to see our scheduled cohort time and depending on the schedule we may or may not have to find the zoom link for class.
      3. Perform (the action sequence) - We first check our eLC to see what cohort time we are assigned to so we must login, click the specified class, and find the schedule. If it is our scheduled cohort time, we have to find the meeting place and start heading out towards the designated location. If not, we must scour for a Zoom link from either our mail or eLC and access the link.
    • Evaluation
      1. Percieve (the state of the world) - Depending on the schedule, we are either at or going to class or waiting for class to start online.
      2. Interpret (the perception) - We found out if we were supposed to attend in-person and went to class or attend online via Zoom.
      3. Compare (the outcome with the goal) - Whether the student or teacher is able to successfully attend class either online or in-person.
  2. Identify your potential users

    3. Students and teachers who are taking/teaching hybrid synchronous classes respectively are affected by this study. Tasks performed would be logging in, seeing the Zoom interface, and accessing the organizational tool consisting of the Zoom links and schedule cohort times for the different courses. Users would benefit such as greater convenience to the user as they wouldn’t have to go search for a Zoom link to join a class. This would also increase engagement of the course from the perspective of professors or instructors by eliminating obstacles students face in joining class online or in-person.

B. Analysis of Existing Solutions

  1. Describe existing solutions

    2. Most professors have sent the Zoom links and information about the cohort times through E-Mail. A pro of this solution is it is guaranteed that the information is always saved in a central location. Another pro of this solution is that there is no need for the student to sign into their eLC account to access the Zoom link and the meeting in the first place. The cons of this solution is that the student would have to tediously search for every individual Zoom link that corresponds to each of their classes.

    Another solution is that professors have posted their Zoom links and cohort meeting times through their eLC announcements page. The pros about this solution is that students can easily navigate and access this information on their eLC page. The cons about this solution is that the student would still need to go to each of their eLC class pages in order to access such information or if the eLC page doesn’t actually have the zoom link or schedule based on the instructor or professor.

  2. Describe potential guidelines and solutions
    1. Strive for consistency - Everything about the solution should be consistent including color scheme to indicate whether it's online or in-person, control scheme , and layout of the interface.
    2. Seek universal usability - Students and Professors can access this interface regardless of what device or platform they are using to view this interface.
    3. Offer informative feedback - Prompting the user to rate the meeting after their meeting ends. Display “connecting” when joining a meeting or “exiting” when leaving a meeting. Indication of a possible cohort time could also be prompted as well.
    4. Keep users in control. - The user has a wide range of actions to customize the interface to their liking. The user could edit the zoom links, and rearrange times.
    5. Reduce short-term memory load - The student and professor’s schedule should contain all relevant information into one interface which includes all the zoom links and cohort times

C. Proposed Solution

  1. Propose a solution

    2. Hybrid Synchronous Classes are more complex than the traditional face-to-face instruction we're used to, so our goal is to ease the complexity for students and instructors in order to facilitate learning. Due to the disorganized nature of how Zoom links and cohort times are being sent to students, the solution to the problem is for students to have access to a Zoom interface that contains the necessary materials and information to be organized in a hybrid-synchronous classroom setting. This interface would include a calendar-like view displaying Zoom links and cohorts information for every course the student is currently registered for. The signifiers would include hyperlinks of the Zoom, buttons to edit the interface or schedule, and blocks on the dates that signify that you can add/delete/edit events or specific dates that might be important. Our solution would afford the user to have a singular place to view their upcoming schedule for the semester, as well as incorporating and accommodating the user virtually and physically.

  2. How will we measure success

    3. Success is measured by how quickly and efficiently a student navigates to their Zoom link and attends the corresponding lecture. We can also measure success by seeing how many students actually use the interface or engage with the interface.
